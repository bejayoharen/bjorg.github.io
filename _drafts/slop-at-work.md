While I love it when my coworkers find ways to be more efficient, I'm starting to see a lot of cases where they are using AI to save their own time, but, unfortunately, end up making more work for others. I think what's happening is that AI often seems like it's magically doing your work for you, so it's easy to forget to make sure it completely solves the problem. After chatting with colleagues at other companies I found that many of them had the same experiences as me.

AI still isn't so great at closing the loop or communicating next steps with coworkers -- it rarely has the complete context you have (and it definitely doesn't know what context your coworkers have) -- so you still need to put some effort into how you share your AI work with coworkers.

Here are some things to think about to help keep you make better use of AI without making your coworkers' day worse:

* **Always check the work AI has done**. For example, if you are using AI to write some linear tickets for you, reread them yourself, so you know the AI did a good job. I've gotten some tickets recently that lacked context, failed to explain priorities, or tell me why the ticket came up (eg, did a customer request it?). I even got one that said "this work is already done", which was a pretty clear sign that the person submitting this ticket did not do any checking at all -- why put a ticket into triage if the work is already done?

* **Make sure your AI understood the assignment**. If someone asks you to contribute some information to a shared table, don't create more work for them by creating a new table with a subset of the information they asked for. Sure AI makes that easy, but you haven't solved the problem, only made more work for your coworker who asked for the info.

* **Don't just dump AI information on other people**. Ask yourself if it solves the problem and reduces the amount of work for everyone. Explain the purpose, edit or refine it so it makes sense in the given context. In short: think of the person who is receiving this information and ask if it will help them, or just confuse them.

* **When communicating, remember you are communicating with humans who might have different context than you or the AI have**. Thinking this way can sometimes be hard even without adding AI slop into the mix, so be sure to give an extra second's thought to make sure the person you are communicating with can make use of the information in a way that's helpful for them. AI can't read minds to understand who your audience is and what they need (yet).

* **Don't share AI plans**. I've seen Linear tickets where people just dump a cursor or Claude plan in with no further explanation, or with the innocent-sounding "@jane, what do you think of this?" I love the planning features of AI tools, but asking others to review these plans is asking others to do your work. You've basically done nothing except prompt an AI and now you're asking your coworkers to do the real work of checking the results.
  * If you are going to use that plan and just want to save it for posterity as a record of the work AI did, say so (although at my job we are discussing if we want to save these in git instead, which seems like a great idea).
  * If you've reviewed the plan but aren't sure if it includes all edge cases, say so, so you have a specific question for your teammates.
  * If you are new to AI and really do want someone else to review the plan and discuss it with you, maybe set a meeting to do that with someone.
  * In most of these cases, you are probably better off just copying and pasting the relevant sections of the plan so others don't have to slog through it all. It's a lot to ask your coworkers to include the plan and have them guess why.

If AI makes your life easier and someone else’s life harder, you are using it wrong.
